The Communication Principle

Local Clients Don't Buy Tactics — They Buy Outcomes

A local plastic surgeon does not care about long-tail keyword expansion or quality score. They care about: "Did the phone ring more this month than last? Are the patients walking in the right kind of patients? Is the spend working?" Your job in every conversation is to translate the work into language tied to those outcomes — without dumbing it down.

💡 The Core Principle

Lead every conversation with what changed for the business — and then show the work that drove it. Lead with tactics and the conversation becomes a vendor update. Lead with outcomes and the conversation becomes a strategic discussion. The work is the same; the framing decides whether you're seen as an agency that runs campaigns or a partner that grows the practice.

Section 1

Translating Channel Metrics Into Business Language

The single most common gap between agency speak and what local clients actually want to hear. These translations are not dumbing down — they're connecting the work to the business outcome.

What We Say (Tactical)
What the Client Hears (Business)
"Our Local Pack visibility improved 22% this month."
"More people in your service area saw your business in Google's map results — at the moment they were ready to call."
"CTR on the Local Geo Modified campaign is up to 6.8%."
"More of the people who saw your ads actually clicked through — meaning our targeting is matching real intent better than before."
"We added 14 new service area pages with unique local content."
"We built dedicated pages for each neighborhood you serve — so when someone in that specific area searches, Google has a clear reason to show them you instead of a competitor."
"GBP profile views increased 38% YoY."
"Almost 40% more people found your Google Business Profile this year than last year — and that profile is the single biggest driver of calls and direction requests from the map."
"Negative keyword list expanded by 87 terms across campaigns."
"We blocked your ads from showing for searches that aren't real buyers for you — which means your budget went further toward the right kind of leads."
"Call conversion volume up 19% on a 4% spend increase."
"You spent slightly more — and you got significantly more calls. The efficiency of your spend improved."
The Rule

Every metric you share should land with: "...which means [outcome for the business]." If you can't finish that sentence cleanly, the metric isn't ready to share yet.

Section 2

Setting Expectations on Small Budgets

A $3K/month account is not going to outrank a regional chain with a $25K/month program in 90 days. Setting expectations correctly at the start of an engagement is what protects the relationship six months in.

⏱️

SEO Takes Time — Be Specific

"Local SEO compounds over 6–12 months. In the first 90 days you should see GBP improvements and on-page foundation work. Ranking gains typically show up in months 3–6 and accelerate after that."

⚡

Paid Search Is Faster But Bounded

"Paid will generate leads in week one. But the ceiling on volume is set by your budget and the search demand in your area — we can optimize efficiency, not invent demand that isn't there."

📈

Lead Quality vs. Volume

"On a tight budget, we'd rather you get 12 highly qualified leads than 50 unqualified ones — because your team's time is also a cost. We'll trade volume for quality and you'll see the difference in your booked-appointment rate."

⚠️ The Promise to Never Make

"We'll get you to #1 in [city]." You don't control Google's algorithm, you don't control your competitors' investment, and you don't control searcher device location. Promising specific rankings is the fastest way to break trust when the inevitable algorithmic volatility hits. Promise process, effort, and the metrics you actually move — not a placement you can't guarantee.

Section 3

The Objections You'll Actually Hear

These come up across almost every local services account. Having a prepared response — grounded in the work we actually do — protects both the client relationship and the program.

My nephew said we should just be doing Facebook ads instead.
How to Respond
"Facebook can be valuable for awareness, especially for visual services like yours. But the searches happening right now — people typing 'med spa near me' or 'facial plastic surgeon Houston' — those are buyers ready to book. Capturing them on Google is higher intent. Facebook works best as an additional layer when budget allows, not a replacement for capturing active demand."
Why aren't we showing up when I search from my house?
How to Respond
"Google personalizes results based on your search history, your location, and even what device you're on. Your own search is the least reliable test because Google knows you own the business. Let's look at the actual ranking data from our tools, which simulate clean searches from different locations in your service area — that's the real picture."
Our competitor is everywhere — they must be spending a fortune. Should we match them?
How to Respond
"Maybe — or maybe they've been at it for 5 years and the visibility you're seeing is the compounding result of that effort. Let's look at where they actually outrank you, why, and what specific moves we can make at your current budget to close the most important gaps first. Matching their spend isn't always the right play; out-targeting them in your highest-intent pockets often is."
I got a bad review last week. Can you make it go away?
How to Respond
"If it violates Google's policies — fake review, off-topic, attack — we'll report it, but removal is inconsistent. The more reliable play is volume: actively gathering new positive reviews so the rating recovers quickly and any single negative gets diluted. One bad review out of 200 is invisible — and a real review acquisition program prevents this from being a recurring problem."
I want to target the whole metro area — Houston, not just Tomball.
How to Respond
"If you genuinely service the whole metro, we can absolutely expand. But here's the trade-off: at your current budget, broader targeting means less efficient spend per city. We typically see hyperlocal terms — like 'pool builders Tomball' — convert significantly better than metro-wide. Let's start with hyperlocal dominance and expand outward as budget and capacity grow."
Can I just have you listen to the calls and tell me which ones are good leads?
How to Respond (for HIPAA clients)
"Because of HIPAA, we don't have access to your call recordings or lead content — that protects both you and your patients. What we can do is track call counts, source attribution, and patterns in the keywords driving calls. The lead-quality conversation works best as a partnership: your intake team flags any trends they're seeing, and we use that signal to adjust targeting accordingly."
Section 4

Communicating Around HIPAA Constraints

For healthcare and legal clients, the lack of access to lead-level content is a real operational gap. How you talk about it shapes whether the client sees it as a limitation or as professional discipline.

🔒 Frame It as Professional Discipline, Not Limitation

When the client asks why you can't review calls or forms, the right framing is: "We don't access PHI because that protects your practice and your patients. We work entirely from aggregated, de-identified performance data — which still gives us everything we need to optimize." This sets you up as a HIPAA-aware partner, not a hamstrung vendor.

🤝 Build the Feedback Loop with the Client's Team

Replace what you can't see with what they can. Ask the intake team monthly: "Any patterns in the lead types — too many of one procedure, too few of another, geography that's off?" Bring this back into the program. The conversation itself becomes a strategic asset.

The Strategic Reframe

For a HIPAA-bound client, your measurement focus shifts from "what did the lead say?" to "what patterns are the program producing, and what feedback are we getting from the practice?" That shift, communicated confidently, is what separates a partner who understands the regulated environment from a vendor who's struggling against it.

Section 5

Review & Reporting Cadence That Works

Most local clients don't have hours to spend in reports. The cadence that earns the most trust is short, focused, and consistent.

TouchpointFrequencyWhat to Cover
Monthly ReportMonthlyTop-line outcomes (leads, calls, GBP actions), what we did, what we're doing next. Keep to 1–2 pages of substance.
Strategy CallMonthly or Quarterly (by tier)30–45 minutes. Lead with business outcomes, walk through priorities, capture client-side feedback on lead quality.
Quick Wins UpdatesAs they happenShort Slack/email when a meaningful improvement lands — new top-5 ranking, big GBP milestone, campaign-level efficiency gain. Keeps the relationship warm between formal touchpoints.
Annual / Bi-Annual Strategy Review1–2x per yearStep back from monthly tactics. Reassess service areas, competitive landscape, budget allocation, and channel mix.
The Reporting Trap to Avoid

Don't send a 40-slide deck full of channel-level metrics to an Essentials 1 client who has 15 minutes. They will not read it, and worse, they'll start to feel overwhelmed by what they're paying for. Match the depth of the report to the time the client actually has — and put the business-outcome translation at the very top, where they'll see it first.

Section 6

Leading the Conversation About Growth

The clients who stay longest and grow into higher tiers are the ones where the agency is proactively shaping the conversation about what's possible next.

📍

Expanding the Service Area

When the current area is performing, propose layering in adjacent towns. New service area pages, new geo-modified campaigns, new GBP service-area updates. This is one of the most natural growth conversations in local.

➕

Adding a Channel

When Paid Search hits diminishing returns at the current budget, that's the moment to introduce Paid Social or Email — not before. Tie the recommendation to a real performance ceiling you're showing in the data.

📈

Tier Upgrade

When the work consistently outpaces the scope of the current tier — more service lines to support, more locations, more sophisticated reporting needed — that's a real upgrade conversation. Frame it as growth, not as needing more money.

Foundations — 5 Core Takeaways
  • Local services is fundamentally different from eCommerce — location IS the intent, not a filter applied after the fact.
  • Identify whether the client is local B2C, local B2B, or hybrid retail (storefront + eComm) before recommending any strategy.
  • Most local clients live in Essentials 1, Essentials 2, Essentials 3, and Professional tiers, with budgets typically $4,000/month or less — concentration beats fragmentation.
  • The four primary channels are SEO, Paid Search, Paid Social (when budget allows), and Email (when it makes sense) — in that order of activation.
  • For HIPAA-bound healthcare and legal clients, we cannot extract lead data or listen to call recordings — we work from aggregated patterns and client-side feedback instead.
2
SEO Considerations — 5 Core Takeaways
  • Local Pack (Google Business Profile / map results) and Local Organic (blue links with local intent) are two different SERP features with different ranking signals — always distinguish them.
  • Google Business Profile is the most influential factor for Local Pack visibility — eligibility, category accuracy, profile completeness, and reviews are the primary levers.
  • Service Area Businesses (SABs) can be configured in GBP without a public address and require dedicated unique-content service area pages to rank for individual towns.
  • Hyperlocal keywords (e.g., "tomball pool builders") almost always outperform broader metro terms ("houston pool builders") when the client genuinely services the hyperlocal area.
  • Active review acquisition is both a Local Pack ranking signal and the most reliable mitigation strategy against the occasional negative review.
3
Paid Considerations — 5 Core Takeaways
  • Use the three-campaign structure: Local Geo Modified (priority, most budget), National Geo Modified (captures off-area intent for in-area locations), and Near Me (device-driven local intent).
  • Build a complete state/region-level negative keyword list at campaign launch — not after spend has already leaked to irrelevant geographies.
  • Pin service to Headline 1 and location to Headline 2; for Near Me ads, remove "Near Me" from the ad text and use the real target market location.
  • Stick with city, town, and region targeting — ZIP-level targeting suppresses volume to the point of starving the campaign.
  • Start with Manual CPC until you have conversion data, then move to Maximize Conversions or Target ROAS; always pair with CallRail tracking numbers per channel.
4
Email Considerations — 5 Core Takeaways
  • Email earns its place — qualify fit using the three-question test (repeat dynamics, real list, tier capacity) before recommending email on any local account.
  • Strong fits include med spa, wellness, fitness, and hybrid retail; weak fits include one-time project services with no list.
  • Core local email programs are monthly newsletter, seasonal promos, appointment reminders, review/referral asks, reactivation, and educational nurture (healthcare).
  • For HIPAA clients, default to general/promotional content and avoid sending Protected Health Information without the proper infrastructure and patient consent.
  • Email's compounding value is in integration — review-request emails feed GBP/SEO, customer match feeds Paid, engaged subscribers feed retargeting.
5
Communicating Strategy — 5 Core Takeaways
  • Local clients don't buy tactics — they buy outcomes. Lead every conversation with what changed for the business, then show the work that drove it.
  • Every metric should land with "...which means [outcome for the business]" — if you can't finish that sentence cleanly, the metric isn't ready to share.
  • Never promise specific rankings or guaranteed positions — you don't control Google's algorithm, competitor investment, or searcher location. Promise process and the metrics you actually move.
  • For HIPAA clients, frame the lack of lead-level access as professional discipline, not limitation; build a feedback loop with the client's intake team to replace what you can't see directly.
  • Match reporting depth to the client's actual time — keep monthly outputs short and outcome-led, save deeper analysis for quarterly or annual strategy reviews.
