Before We Start

The Mindset Reset

The single most important shift to make before you touch a local services account.

💡 Core Principle

Local SEO and Local Paid are not just "regular SEO and PPC with a city name slapped on." They are fundamentally different from national or eCommerce work because the user's location is the intent. When someone searches "facial plastic surgeon near me" or "pool builder Tomball," the geography is not a filter — it's the core of what they need. The most common hang-up for people new to this vertical is defaulting to high-level optimization (technical SEO, brand campaigns, broad keyword targeting) and missing the actual way users search for local businesses: business type + location.

🎯

What You Do Need

  • How users search for local services (intent patterns)
  • The difference between Local Pack and Local Organic
  • Google Business Profile fundamentals and eligibility
  • The right campaign structure for Local Paid
  • Why location modifiers beat broad keywords
  • How to read budgets and tier constraints quickly
🚫

What You Don't Need

  • Deep expertise in any specific service trade
  • A medical or legal degree (for healthcare/legal clients)
  • National keyword strategy playbooks
  • Enterprise-level paid budgets to make impact
  • To talk to clients about HIPAA-protected lead data
  • An eCommerce mindset (this is not online retail)
🔑

Your Competitive Edge

Most local businesses have small marketing teams, limited time, and inconsistent agency experiences. They want a partner who understands how their specific local market behaves. Speaking the language of local search — Local Pack, GBP, service area pages, geo-modified campaigns — instantly elevates you above generalists who treat every account the same.

Also Part of the Mindset Reset

Local Services Is Not eCommerce — And It Should Never Be Treated Like It

A common early misstep is dropping eCommerce tactics (broad targeting, "shop now" CTAs, national reach, demand gen) onto local accounts. Local paid media is a digital marketing strategy using location targeting and keyword modifiers to reach an audience within specific geographic areas — and we should only utilize a local strategy if the client is contractually obligated to target a specific location or if they are providing a service with location constraints.

⚠️ Critical Mindset Shift

If a client has an eComm website, they can sell nationwide and should not be using this strategy. But for a med spa, a pool builder, a plastic surgeon, or a wellness center — the user's location IS their intent. The location the user is searching for indicates the user's intent (the search defines the user's intent). Treating these clients with eCommerce tactics — broad national reach, generic ad copy, no location modifiers — wastes their already-limited budget and misses the high-intent traffic that actually converts.

❌ eCommerce / National Language — Avoid for Local
These approaches assume nationwide reach and online transactions
  • "Shop our full collection"
  • National keyword targeting with no geo modifier
  • Broad demand gen campaigns on small budgets
  • "Free shipping over $X" messaging
  • Targeting the entire US for a local business
  • Ignoring Google Business Profile
  • Generic landing pages with no city/neighborhood context
✅ Local-First Language — Use This Instead
These approaches match how local users actually search and convert
  • "Serving [City] since [Year]"
  • Geo-modified keywords (service + city/town)
  • "Near Me" campaigns with localized targeting
  • "Book Your Consultation" / "Schedule a Visit"
  • City- and neighborhood-specific landing pages
  • Active Google Business Profile management
  • "Request a Quote" with location context

Section 1

What "Local Services" Actually Means at OBx

A local services client at OBx is a business that serves a specific geographic region — either from a physical storefront, a service area footprint, or both. They are not selling products nationally online. Their growth comes from being found by the right person, in the right place, at the moment they need a service.

1

Geographic Service Constraint

The client either has a physical location customers come to (med spa, dental office, gym) or visits customer locations within a defined service area (pool builders, landscapers, contractors). They cannot meaningfully serve a customer 1,000 miles away.

2

Conversion = Lead, Not Online Sale

Success is measured in phone calls, form fills, appointment requests, and consultations — not eCommerce transactions. We're getting them to the door; their sales/intake team handles the close.

3

Reputation & Reviews Are Core Currency

Word of mouth and online reputation drive a disproportionate amount of business. Reviews, ratings, and Google Business Profile signals carry as much weight as keyword rankings.

4

Smaller Budgets, Sharper Strategy

Most local clients fall in the lower tiers ($4K/month or less). Every dollar has to work harder. Tight campaign structure, smart geo-targeting, and disciplined negative keywords matter more here than anywhere else.

The Local Client Filter

A local SEO/Paid approach is intended for businesses contractually obligated to serve specific areas or that genuinely operate within those defined locations. Don't "localize" a site or campaign just because a client happens to get most of their work in one area — if they have national capacity and want national leads, that's a different strategy entirely. Always confirm the service-area reality before recommending local-first tactics.

Section 2

B2C, B2B & Hybrid Local — Know Your Client

Local services break into three patterns. Each one changes how you target, how you write copy, and what conversions matter. Local paid strategy applies to both B2B and B2C businesses (e.g., Roofers, Doctors, Installation, Pool Builders, etc.).

👥 Local B2C

Consumer-facing local services

Who They Are

  • Med spas, plastic surgeons, dental, wellness
  • Personal services (salons, gyms, fitness studios)
  • Home services for homeowners (pool builders, landscapers)

Buyer Pattern

  • Single individual makes the decision
  • Often emotional + research-driven
  • Reviews, photos, before/after content matter a lot

Primary Conversion

  • Phone call to book
  • Online consultation request
  • Walk-in / appointment scheduling

🏢 Local B2B

Business-facing local services

Who They Are

  • Commercial contractors, B2B installers
  • Local equipment service / repair
  • Commercial cleaning, commercial landscaping

Buyer Pattern

  • Procurement manager, facilities lead, or owner
  • Longer evaluation, more formal RFQ
  • Vendor reliability and references weigh heavily

Primary Conversion

  • Quote / estimate request
  • Phone call to discuss scope
  • Site visit scheduling

🏬 Hybrid (Local + eComm)

Larger budget exception cases

Who They Are

  • Retail with physical storefronts AND eComm presence
  • Example: Johnnie-O (apparel with retail locations)
  • NOT pure eCommerce without storefronts

Buyer Pattern

  • Both walk-in retail shoppers and online buyers
  • Often larger budgets — can play in Demand Gen, Meta, TikTok, Merchant Center

Primary Conversion

  • Online sale + in-store visit
  • Both transactional and footfall metrics matter
Important Distinction

Hybrid (retail + eComm with physical locations) ≠ pure eComm. A client like Johnnie-O has real storefronts and can support broader channel mixes including Meta, TikTok, Demand Gen, and Merchant Center on top of standard Google Search. But a retail eCommerce client with no physical, standalone storefront is a national eCommerce account — that belongs in a different vertical playbook entirely. Always confirm: do they have physical service-area locations or not?

Tier & Budget Reality

Most local services clients live in the lower OBx tiers. Knowing this up front shapes what tactics are realistic, what channels are in scope, and how you set client expectations.

Section 5

How Local Users Actually Search

The biggest gap between new teammates and experienced local-vertical strategists is understanding the real search behavior. Local users do not search the way national/eComm users search.

The Three Local Search Patterns

📍

Geo-Modified Searches

"facial plastic surgeon Houston"

The user explicitly types a location. Very high intent — they know what they want and where. These are your highest-priority keywords.

🧭

"Near Me" Searches

"med spa near me"

User relies on device location. Intent is local but they haven't named the city. Google interprets location automatically.

🔍

Implicit Local Searches

"pool builders" (searched from a phone in Tomball)

No geo modifier, but Google infers local intent from device location. Often returns Local Pack results anyway.

Hyperlocal Beats Metro

Real Example

If your client is located in Tomball, then "tomball pool builders" is a stronger target than "houston pool builders" — even though Tomball is part of the greater Houston area. Why? Because the user typing "tomball pool builders" is signaling much higher local intent and is more likely to actually convert with a Tomball-area provider. Don't reach for the bigger metro name unless the client genuinely services the entire area.

Geo-Located vs. Geo-Modified Keywords

An important distinction from local SEO research that shapes how we evaluate competitors and rankings.

Keyword TypeDefinitionExample
Geo-locatedKeywords tracked from a specific location, i.e. vegan pizza searched from the University District in Seattle, WA. Depending on the competitiveness of your market, you may have different competitors for each geo-located keyword search."med spa" tracked from a phone in Belage's neighborhood
Geo-modifiedKeywords with location modifiers included in the phrase, i.e. vegan pizza Seattle. Your competitors for each geo-modified keyword can also vary from search to search in competitive markets."med spa Houston" — the location is in the search string itself
Why This Matters

Every geographic market you serve is subject to different competitors. To dominate your markets, you should analyze and track the unique competitive landscape for each one. Your client's "competitors" change depending on where the searcher is located — so a single rank tracker number doesn't tell the full story. Build the habit of thinking about ranking in terms of "where is the searcher when they see us?" not just "what position are we?"

Section 6

Primary Channels & What Earns Budget

In the local services vertical, four channels carry the load — but they don't all show up on every account. Tier and budget determine which ones get activated.

🔍

SEO — The Foundation

Always in scope. Local SEO is the backbone of every account in this vertical. Includes Google Business Profile optimization, location/service pages, on-page signals, and reputation management.

Why first: Compounds over time, drives non-paid traffic, supports the Local Pack which is often the most clicked SERP feature for local intent.

💰

Paid Search — The Volume Lever

In scope for Essentials 2+ in most cases. Google Ads (and Microsoft Ads) targeting geo-modified, near-me, and national-geo campaigns to capture high-intent searches.

Why second: Fastest way to generate leads when SEO is still maturing. Tight campaign structure is critical at low budgets.

📱

Paid Social — When Budget Allows

Typically Professional tier and up. Meta (Facebook/Instagram) for awareness, reach, and lead generation in local geographies.

When it works: Visual services (med spa, plastic surgery, aesthetic), community-driven brands, retail with storefronts. Less ROI for pure transactional service searches.

✉️

Email — When It Makes Sense

Selective use. Email shines for businesses with repeat-customer dynamics — wellness, med spa memberships, recurring services — and businesses with a healthy existing list.

When to skip: One-time purchase services with no list, or clients without the bandwidth to maintain a sender reputation.

💡 Budget Allocation Principle

For most lower-tier local accounts, the practical mix is: SEO + Paid Search first. Paid Social and Email are added as budget grows or when the client's business model clearly supports them (visual services for Social, repeat-customer dynamics for Email). Never spread a small budget thin across four channels — concentration beats fragmentation for local accounts.

Section 7

HIPAA & Compliance Considerations

Several of our local services clients operate in regulated industries — particularly healthcare (med spa, plastic surgery, wellness) and legal. These require specific operational adjustments.

⚠️ Critical Compliance Rule

For healthcare and legal clients with HIPAA or attorney-client privilege requirements: we cannot extract lead data and we cannot listen to phone call recordings. This means tools that would normally help us learn from real conversations and form submissions (CallRail transcript review, LOOP-level form content inspection) have to be used differently. We can see that a call or form happened — we cannot review the content.

🏥

Healthcare Clients (HIPAA)

Med spas, plastic surgeons, wellness clinics, dental offices. Any time the lead/call may contain Protected Health Information (PHI), we are restricted on what we can access and store.

⚖️

Legal Clients

Attorneys and law firms. Calls and forms may contain privileged information about a potential case. Same operational principle: no listening to recordings, no extracting lead detail.

🧰

What We Can Still Do

Track conversion counts, source attribution, keyword/campaign performance, GBP insights, page-level engagement, and aggregated form/call volume. We measure outcomes — we just don't inspect the contents.

Strategic Implication

Because we lose the ability to review specific lead content for these clients, we have to lean more heavily on signal patterns — keyword themes generating calls, landing pages with high engagement, campaign-level conversion rates — and on client-side feedback. Build a habit of asking the client (or their intake team) periodically: "Are the leads coming through the right type for your practice? Any patterns we should adjust toward or away from?" That conversation replaces the transcript review we'd do on a non-HIPAA account.

Section 8

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The patterns that trip up people new to this vertical. Knowing them in advance saves you (and the client) months of misdirection.

✗ Don't Do This

Focusing on high-level technical SEO optimization while ignoring how users actually search for local businesses. Core Web Vitals and schema are important — but they're not what wins local. Local users search for business type + location, and if you don't have pages and campaigns built around that, you're invisible to the actual buyer.

✓ Do This Instead

Lead with the keyword pattern "service + city/town" across SEO and Paid. Build location pages, geo-modified campaigns, and a strong Google Business Profile. Technical work supports this — it doesn't replace it.

✗ Don't Do This

Running Paid Search with broad keywords and no location modifiers. A campaign targeting "med spa" with US-wide targeting will burn a $4K budget in days and deliver nothing. This is the single most common Paid Search mistake on new local accounts.

✓ Do This Instead

Use the OBx local campaign structure: Local Geo Modified, National Geo Modified, and Near Me campaigns with tight location targeting and proactive negative keywords. Part 3 covers this in full.

✗ Don't Do This

Skipping local landing pages. Sending all traffic to a generic home page or services page means you have nothing telling Google (or the user) that you serve their specific town. Rankings and ad relevance both suffer.

✓ Do This Instead

Build dedicated area-served pages for each city/town the client genuinely services. Each one needs unique content about the business's experience in that area — not just a duplicated template with the city name swapped.

✗ Don't Do This

Confusing Google Business Profile (Local Pack) results with Local Organic results. They are two different SERP features, ranked by different signals. Telling a client "we'll improve your rankings" without specifying which one creates expectation mismatches.

✓ Do This Instead

Always distinguish the two in client conversations. Local Pack = the map results, driven by GBP and physical location. Local Organic = the standard blue links with local intent, driven by on-page signals and authority. Part 2 covers the full distinction.

✗ Don't Do This

Applying eCommerce or national playbooks to local clients. Demand Gen on a $3K Essentials 1 budget. Broad shopping campaigns. National keyword targets. None of these match how local buyers behave.

✓ Do This Instead

Match the playbook to the model. Local-first tactics for local clients. Save the broader plays for the hybrid retail accounts that actually have the budget and the storefront network to support them.

✗ Don't Do This

Asking to listen to call recordings on a HIPAA client. Even with good intent, requesting access to call content for a healthcare client puts the client (and OBx) in a compliance bind.

✓ Do This Instead

Know which of your clients carry compliance constraints before you ask any question about lead data. For those clients, work with conversion counts, source attribution, and client-side feedback on lead quality instead.
